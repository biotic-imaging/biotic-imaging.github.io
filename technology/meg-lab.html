Our History

The Laboratory for Clinical Magnetoencephalography (MEG) (MEG Lab) has been a part of the IWK Health Centre for over 7 years and is central to the local neuroimaging community. Managed by the BIOTIC, the MEG Lab houses the only MEG system east of Montreal, an Elekta Neuromag Vector View 306-channel whole head system.

What is MEG?

MEG is a passive, non-invasive neuroimaging modality that measures neuronal activity in the brain using extremely sensitive sensors. This means that the system doesn’t do anything to the participant so they don’t feel anything during the scan. More importantly, MEG is completely harmless, even for those with metal inside of their bodies. Due to the nature of MEG, an abundance of research has been conducted across all age ranges in both healthy controls and patient populations. Below you’ll see a list of clinical research applications utilized by MEG (e.g. epilepsy, Alzheimer’s, traumatic brain injury, etc.).

Within our lab, simultaneous EEG and MEG measurements can also be conducted using high-density EEG systems (64-channel), and physiological signals such as EMG, EOG, and EKG can be simultaneously recorded. Various stimuli can be presented to the participant including auditory, visual, or somatosensory stimulation. The system can record data in the supine or seated position, proving it to be very versatile and open to numerous research applications.

How MEG Works

Active neurons within the brain produce a changing magnetic field. Our Neromag system uses super conducting quantum interference devices (SQUIDS) to measure this neural activity from outside the head with millisecond time resolution. We can then infer from the activity across sensors, precisely where this activity is coming from (within millimetres).

Advantages of MEG

MEG has both a higher temporal resolution and higher spatial resolution than several of the currently used neuroimaging modalities. The temporal resolution of MEG is orders of magnitude higher than functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) and positron emission tomography (PET). In contrast to electroencephelpgraphy (EEG) signals, which are strongly influenced by inhomogeneities in the head and poor conductivity of the scalp, the magnetic fields measured by MEG are produced mainly by current flow in the relatively homogenous intracranial space and pass through the scalp largely unaltered, permitting spatial localization (i.e. where the activity is coming from inside the brain) with millisecond time resolution.

In additional to its technical abilities, MEG is harmless and also silent, so compared to an MRI it’s a rather quiet and comfortable experience for participants and patients.



MEG ResearchOur capabilities and interests

The Laboratory for Clinical MEG is one of two MEG laboratories in Canada that perform clinical MEG scans on patients with intractable epilepsy. The data is used to localize epileptic activity, thus, providing useful information to the epileptologists and neurosurgeons prior to conducting resection surgery. To date over 35 epilepsy patients have been scanned at IWK for this purpose.

The MEG system at the IWK is also used in various research projects in healthy individuals as well as patients. Studies currently being conducted in healthy subjects include visual processing, auditory processing, somatosensory processing, motor function, inhibition and sleep studies. Clinical research studies include bipolar disorder, Lupus disease traumatic brain injury, concussions and epilepsy, with more clinical studies expected in the near future. Around the world, MEG has also been used to study stroke, Alzheimer’s disease, Parkinson’s disease, obsessive compulsive disorder, attention deficit disorder, psychosis as well as visual and auditory impairments.

Dr. Maher QuraanMEG Lab Scientific Lead   

Dr. Quraan is the lead magnetoencephalography (MEG) researcher for BIOTIC and a Research Scientist with the IWK Health Centre. Dr. Quraan’s research is focused on 1) developing and assessing the performance of advanced computational models using simulations and empirical data, including spatiotemporal dynamics, functional connectivity and network measures, and 2) applications of these models to understand healthy brain function as well as brain dysfunction in various neurological and psychiatric disorders at the system and network levels.
