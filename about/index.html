About us

BIOTIC is an imaging centre with collaborators around the world. We continue to build our network of expertise and are constantly pushing the development of technologies that will improve the lives of patients globally.

BIOTIC (BIOmedical Translational Imaging Centre) is a multi-site imaging centre that is embedded in the two leading research and teaching hospitals in Nova Scotia. Our multidisciplinary and cross-functional teams, providing expertise in all facets of imaging research and development, collaborate on commercial development projects with industry partners as well as research and development projects with a number of institutions. Our equipment, including a 306-sensor MEG, high field MRI, EEG, TMS, and pre-clinical PET/CT and MRI, are housed in three labs, in two health centres encompassing over 12,000 square feet of lab space.

The BIOTIC team co-develops medical technologies as well as creates new imaging techniques and methods for diagnosing and monitoring treatment. We are the only group in Atlantic Canada with a collection of research-dedicated imaging technologies of this magnitude. The need to have a research-dedicated imaging facility with a multi-disciplinary staff capable of undertaking research to result in commercialization has lead to the creation of BIOTIC. This was made possible due to a partnership between IWK Health Centre and QEII Health Sciences Centre, and the region’s life sciences community.

BIOTIC has become a vital part of Nova Scotia’s life sciences ecosystem. Companies are routinely capitalizing on BIOTIC’s location and equipment, in combination with the team’s depth of knowledge, range of expertise and passion for advancing science and technology. This is what truly sets BIOTIC apart.

Dr. Patrick McGrath, Integrated Vice President Research and Innovation at the IWK and Nova Scotia Health Authority

Dr. Steven Beyea
  Science Lead
As the scientific lead for BIOTIC, Dr. Beyea is responsible for directing the overall research program of a diverse team of scientists, with the goal of improving Canadian healthcare through researching and commercializing diagnostic imaging technologies. He is also a Research Scientist at the IWK and an Associate Professor at Dalhousie University, where he teaches medical imaging at both the undergraduate and graduate level, in addition to supervision of graduate and undergraduate students. Steven received his PhD in physics in 2000, and since 2003 has lead a medical physics research program in Halifax that focuses on developing novel image acquisition and analysis technologies that improve the diagnosis of human disease and rapidly translating them into the clinical setting.

Dr. Sharon Clarke
  Clinician/Scientist
Dr. Sharon Clarke completed the combined MD/PhD program at the University of Western Ontario, obtaining her PhD in Medical Biophysics in 2004 and her MD in 2005. She attended Dalhousie University for her residency in Diagnostic Radiology, and then completed a one-year fellowship at Stanford University in Body MRI. Dr. Sultan Darvesh
  Affiliated Scientist
Dr. Darvesh is a behavioural neurologist and trained organic chemist with interest in medicinal chemistry. An affiliated scientist with BIOTIC, his research has been instrumental in determining the importance and expression of butyrylcholinesterase in the human brain. His research focus is the development of therapeutic and diagnostic agents for Alzheimer’s disease, with butyrylcholinesterase as the target. Dr Darvesh is also a co-founder of Treventis Corporation and is using BIOTIC's preclinical imaging technologies to test a novel diagnostic for Alzheimer's disease.
Dr. Tim Bardouille
  Research Scientist
Dr. Bardouille is the lead magnetoencephalography (MEG) researcher for BIOTIC. He is a Research Scientist with the IWK Health Centre, and an Adjunct Professor with the School of Physiotherapy and Department of Computer Sciences at Dalhousie University. Dr. Bardouille’s research interests focus on developing mathematical and computation approaches to measuring synchrony and communication in the human brain. He applies these approaches for the purposes of clinical diagnosis and the tracking of treatment efficacy (e.g., stroke rehabilitation).
