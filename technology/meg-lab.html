Laboratory for Clinical Magnetoencephalography

Non-invasive neuroimaging at the IWK Health Centre

Our History

The Laboratory for Clinical Magnetoencephalography (MEG) has been a part of the IWK Health Centre for over 7 years and has been a major resource to the local neuroimaging community ever since. The lab houses the only MEG system east of Montreal, an Elekta Neuromag Vector View 306-channel whole head system.

What is MEG?

MEG is a non-invasive neuroimaging modality that measures the magnetic fields generated by neural activity in the brain using extremely sensitive sensors. The Neromag system uses super conducting quantum interference devices (SQUIDS) capable of measuring neural activity from outside the head with millisecond time resolution, orders of magnitude higher than functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) and positron emission tomography (PET). In contrast to electroencephelpgraphy (EEG) signals, which are strongly influenced by inhomogeneities in the head and poor conductivity of the scalp, the magnetic fields measured by MEG are produced mainly by current flow in the relatively homogenous intracranial space and pass through the scalp largely unaltered, permitting spatial localization (i.e. where the activity is coming from inside the brain) with millisecond time resolution.

Simultaneous EEG and MEG measurements can also be conducted using high density EEG systems, and physiological signals such as EMG, EOG, and EKG can be simultaneously recorded. Various stimuli can be presented to the participant including auditory, visual, or somatosensory stimulation. The system can record data in the supine or seated position.

MEG ResearchOur capabilities and interests

The Laboratory for Clinical MEG is one of two MEG laboratories in Canada that perform clinical MEG scans on patients with intractable epilepsy. The data is used to localize epileptic activity, thus, providing useful information to the epileptologists and neurosurgeons prior to conduction resection surgery. To date 35 (?) epilepsy patients have been scanned at IWK for this purpose.

The MEG system at the IWK is also used in various research projects in healthy individuals as well as patients. Studies currently being conducted in healthy subjects include visual processing, auditory processing, somatosensory processing, motor function, inhibition and sleep studies. Clinical studies include bipolar disorder, Lupus disease traumatic brain injury, concussions and epilepsy, with more clinical studies expected in the near future. Around the world, MEG has also been used to study stroke, Azheimer’s disease, Parkinson’s disease, obsessive compulsive disorder, attention deficit disorder, psychosis as well as visual and auditory impairments.

Dr. Maher QuraanMEG Lab Scientific Lead   

Dr. Quraan is the lead magnetoencephalography (MEG) researcher for BIOTIC and a Research Scientist with the IWK Health Centre. Dr. Quraan’s research is focused on 1) developing and assessing the performance of advanced computational models using simulations and empirical data, including spatiotemporal dynamics, functional connectivity and network measures, and 2) applications of these models to understand healthy brain function as well as brain dysfunction in various neurological and psychiatric disorders at the system and network levels.

Ron BishopMEG Imaging Technologist & Research Coordinator   

Ron joined the BIOTIC team in the summer of 2014. He is responsible for the daily quality assurance and operation of the MEG. He recently received his MSc. Rehabilitation Research degree from Dalhousie University, where he gained extensive experience with MEG experimental design, recording, and data analysis.

Dr. Tim BardouilleAffiliated Scientist   

Dr. Bardouille is an Assistant Professor at Dalhousie University's Department of Physics & Atmospheric Science, the lead of the Biosignal Lab, and an affiliated scientist with the IWK Health Centre. Dr. Bardouille’s research interests focus on developing mathematical and computation approaches to measuring synchrony and communication in the human brain. He applies these approaches for the purposes of clinical diagnosis and the tracking of treatment efficacy (e.g., stroke rehabilitation).
